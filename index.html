Electric ATV

This project involves the conversion of a traditional 110CC gasoline-powered ATV into a fully electric vehicle. The transformation leverages modern electric vehicle (EV) technology, including a custom-built 48V battery and an electric motor, to create an eco-friendly and efficient mode of transportation. The project showcases the integration of new technologies with existing mechanical systems, demonstrating a hands-on approach to innovation and sustainability.

Electric ATV

Overview

The primary goal of this project is to explore the feasibility and benefits of converting a small off-road vehicle to electric power. By replacing the internal combustion engine with an electric motor and battery system, the project aims to reduce emissions, improve energy efficiency, and enhance the vehicle’s performance. Additionally, the project serves as a learning experience, allowing for the development of skills in areas such as battery construction, electrical system integration, and mechanical design. This project is a step towards understanding the intricacies of electric vehicles, contributing to the growing field of sustainable transportation. Through this conversion, the project also aims to inspire others to explore similar innovations in their own vehicles or machines.

The Objectives

In the full conversion, there are three main goals:

• Building the Battery
• Adding the Electric Motor
• Modifying the current system to add motor braking and reversing

With the completion of these goals, the ATV will run solely on electric power.

Part Selection

While selecting the necessary components, I had to consider several key factors such as weight of the rider and ATV itself, the speed and torque required from the motor, the battery and BMS that would sufficiently power the motor and implementation of the other functionalities. For the motor, I ended up choosing the Vevor motor which was a fairly cost effective (under $160) and with decent power 1800 rpm. Furthermore, the motor comes with its own speed controller which is incredibly useful in moderating power and various functions for the motor. For the battery, I chose standard 18650 cells, in a 13 series and 7 parallel configuration since they are commonly used for building battery packs. Additionally, I chose a rather high tolerance Battery Managment System (BMS), with max amperage at 50A, a lot higher than the 33A required for the motor. The ATV frame chosen was a cheap 110cc ATV that was being discarded and sold for parts, but luckily came with the frame, steering and axles intact.

The Battery

The pictures show my 48.1V battery built with 18650 cells in a 7 parallel, 13 series configuration. It should run for 24 minutes at full throttle, pending tests. The red wires on the top left are for power and charging, and the bottom right wire is ground. I designed and 3D printed the brackets. The BMS regulates current, prevents overcharging, and handles up to 50 amps, while my motor controller uses 33 amps. The side view shows wire connections through a ribbon cable to the BMS, alternating through all positive terminals and connecting to the negative terminal. The connections were formed using nickel strips and a spot welder.

Problems Along The Way

The conversion was easier said than done, with quite a few issues along the way. First was a charger with lower capacity than the battery, with a max charge at 42V compared to the max charge for my battery at 54.6V. Therefore, the testing for the motor was done using nominal voltages at around 48V. Second, and a major issue, was fitting a new sprocket on the motor to fit the chain of the old rear axle assembly. I was not able to find a pre-made part, which meant designing and getting the piece custome made. Then an issue with mounting the motor, as it was vastly different from the original engine, and a temporary wooden mount showed to be too brittle.

Hand Gesture Puppet

This project showcases a sophisticated integration of MediaPipe, computer vision, and robotics to create an interactive system that translates hand gestures into physical actions. By employing a custom-designed 3D-printed robotic hand and interfacing it with an Arduino board, the system enables real-time gesture mimicry, offering a novel approach to human-computer interaction.

Hand Gesture Puppet

Overview

This project integrates MediaPipe and computer vision technologies to recognize hand gestures and translate them into physical movements using a 3D-printed robotic hand. By leveraging gesture recognition to interface with an Arduino board, the project aims to create an interactive and responsive robotic hand that mimics human gestures.

The Objectives

The goal was to understand the workings of the neural network to not only train different gestures, but be able to incorporate arduino communication with a microcontroller. The two main goals were:

• Robotic Hand Design: Design and 3D-print a robotic hand capable of replicating various gestures.
• Arduino Integration: Develop an interface between the gesture recognition system and an Arduino board to control the robotic hand.

Decision Details

I was able to use a repository already integrated MediaPipe which was open source. A solid chunk of time was spent in understanding the functionality of the neural network and how I could use the recognized hand gestures and send signals to the microcontroller. Then came the design choices to create a hand from scratch that could mimic the gestures accurately. I decided to build individual fingers that were held straight through an elastic string, and bent using normal string. The tension from the elastic string would restraighten the finer after the servo returned to its original position. For this first iteration, I decided to go with the Arduino Uno R3 simply for its extensive community and female header pins for ease of attachment.

Problems Along The Way

The largest issue was improving finger bending from the 180 degree servo rotation, and a better use of the elastic tension so the finger would straighten properly every time. Furthermore, I also needed to reduce the friction from the joints, and prevent the strings getting stuck between the joints. The servos I was using also were jittering a lot initially and I discovered that there wasn't sufficient power provided for the servos and Arduino. I had to then supplement the servos with additional batteries.

Future Additions

I plan on working on the rest of the hand as well so it is not static, and moves as a palm would. I also want to improve the finger rotation by changing the current assembly. Also, you may have noticed the FPS significantly drops as my hand enters the view (it is very brief, but drops from 30 FPS to under 3). This is due to the way I setup the arduino communication with the neural network and another thing I plan on improving on. Lastly, I want to make the entire system independent of connection by using an ESP-32 with Camera capabilites, as that microcontroller also has wifi and bluetooth capabilites.
